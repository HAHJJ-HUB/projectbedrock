*Version 1.0*

---

## What this document is

The Casefilers' public-facing copy and pitch, organized for use across the marketing site, the press kit, pitch decks, partnership outreach, and the access-request flow. Every line of copy here has been considered for *voice* and *register*. Do not rewrite without consulting the Oracle Voice Guide.

---

## 1. One-line description

> **The Casefilers.** A four-filer underground public-source intelligence unit.

This is the single line you put on your homepage, your X bio, your business card, your "what do you do?" reply at parties. It works because:

- *The Casefilers* — definite article, established unit
- *four-filer* — small, specific, not a platform
- *underground* — implies access to material others miss
- *public-source* — legal, defensible, not surveillance
- *intelligence unit* — the work category, said plainly

It is also true. Every word does work.

## 3. The trust signals

Used directly under the hero, and again in the footer:

> **By invitation. No public log. Built for the record.**

Three short phrases, separated by full stops. Each is doing distinct work:

- *By invitation* — premium, selective, scarcity-positioned
- *No public log* — privacy, discretion, you're not adding to a database
- *Built for the record* — for professionals who care about the record's integrity

These three phrases should appear together. Do not split them across surfaces — they earn their weight as a triad.

## 5. The "How it works" section

Four steps. Roman numerals. The institutional voice.

```
I.    Name the subject.
      Precise or broad. The unit takes it as a brief and convenes.

II.   The room is sealed.
      Infiltrator and Ghost go to work. Nexus pins the board. Oracle reads along.

III.  The case is handed up.
      When the field work is done, the unit hands everything to Oracle. She writes.

IV.   The file is bound.
      A signed case file. Finding, contradictions, exhibits, source inventory. Yours.
```

This four-step structure mirrors the actual procedure of a real intelligence brief: subject, working, drafting, binding. The roman numerals are doing the work of saying *this is a procedure, not a feature list*.

## 9. The two CTA buttons — what they mean

**REQUEST ACCESS ↗** — primary CTA. Opens a brief access-request form. Three or four questions: who you are, what kind of work you do, the subject of your first intended case. The unit reviews each request. Not everyone is admitted. Those who are receive an invitation by email.

**READ A SAMPLE FILE** — secondary CTA. Opens a fully built sample case file. This must be a *real* file — not a marketing mock-up. It is the single most important conversion asset on the site. Anyone who reads a complete sample case file will either be sold or will leave permanently. There is no middle outcome.

The sample file should be on a subject the user can verify against the public record — so they can check the unit's work themselves and find it sound.

## 11. Press kit — talking points

If you are interviewed about the product, the talking points are:

1. **"The product is not framed as software."** The user opens a case, the unit convenes, the room seals, the file is bound. The metaphor is the product.

2. **"We do not use the word 'agent.'"** The four filers are filers. Calling them agents reduces them to features.

3. **"We do not summarise. We reconstruct."** The case file is a multi-voice document. Every claim is cited. Every contradiction is on record. Every recovered source is preserved.

4. **"It is by invitation because the unit's time is finite."** This is true. Selectivity is a property of the unit's pace, not a sales gimmick.

5. **"Public-source means everything we reach is something the public has the right to read."** The work is legal, defensible, journalism-grade.

**Do not say:**
- *"It's an AI tool"* — say *"It is a software product, but it is operated as a unit"*
- *"Powered by GPT/Claude/etc."* — implementation is back-end
- *"For everyone"* — it is not for everyone
- *"Trusted by [logos]"* — name customers only with their permission and only when it lifts the product's seriousness

## 13. Three things the marketing site must do

Beyond the copy: the marketing site has three jobs. Get these right and the copy carries itself.

**1. Look like the product.** Same warm dark palette. Same brass accents. Same italic serif voice. Same letterhead. A prospect should not be able to tell where the marketing site ends and the product begins. *This is the conversion mechanism.*

**2. Show the sample file.** Make it impossible to leave the site without having either requested access or read a complete sample file. The sample file does the selling.

**3. Demonstrate restraint.** No animated gradients, no chatbot popups, no email-capture modals, no cookie banners larger than the hero, no "we're trusted by..." logo wall. The marketing site is the first proof that the brand keeps its promises.

## 14. Where to *not* market

Some channels are off-brand and should be avoided:

- General consumer AI marketplaces (App Store featured listings, Product Hunt)
- AI tool review sites that lump us in with chatbots and copilots
- LinkedIn "AI agents revolution" thought-leadership posts
- Cold email campaigns to general professionals

Some channels are on-brand and worth investing in:

- Investigative journalism conferences (IRE, Logan Symposium, Dataharvest)
- OSINT communities (Bellingcat, NixIntel, Trace Labs)
- Niche newsletters read by the target audience (Defector, The Bureau of Investigative Journalism, Coda Story)
- Direct outreach to investigative units at major outlets
- Partnerships with journalism schools, fact-checking nonprofits, and FOIA advocacy groups
