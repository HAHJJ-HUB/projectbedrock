# The Casefilers — Brand Bible

*Version 1.0*

---

## What this document is

This is the canonical source of truth for everything the Casefilers brand is and is not. Every other document — the voice guide, the illustrator brief, the marketing copy, the engineering handoff — descends from this one. When they disagree, this document is correct.

---

## 1. The product, in one sentence

The Casefilers is a four-filer underground public-source intelligence unit that opens cases, reaches archived and dissolved material across the public internet, marks contradictions, detects narrative drift, and delivers a signed case file.

It is software. It is never framed as software.

---

## 2. What we are selling

We are not selling an AI agent platform. We are not selling a research tool. We are not selling a search engine. We are selling **admission to a unit**.

The user opens a case. The unit convenes. The room seals. The unit works. The file is bound. The user reads the finding.

The metaphor is not decoration. It is the product.

---

## 3. The tagline

> **Reconstruct the record. Build the case.**

Two verbs, two objects, parallel structure. *Reconstruct* implies the record exists but is broken. *Build* implies a case is assembled deliberately, item by item, like a real legal or intelligence brief. Both words are non-negotiable.

The supporting line:

> The Casefilers do not run searches. They open cases. They activate the unit. They build the file.

Three short sentences, three verbs, escalating. *Open. Activate. Build.* This is the core marketing rhythm.

---

## 4. The four filers

Each filer has a role, a desk, and a place in the work. They are members of a unit, not a cast of characters. They do not have personalities in the consumer sense. They have *functions* and *voices*.

| Filer | Role line (canonical) | What they do |
|---|---|---|
| **Infiltrator** | finds what search misses | Works the public web aggressively — operators, APIs, government dumps, GitHub, paste sites, mailing lists. The filer who *goes in*. |
| **Ghost** | recovers what disappeared | Wayback Machine, defunct forums, leaked dumps, deleted press releases, dissolved subdirectories. Returns with material others have let go. |
| **Nexus** | connects the record | Pins entities, builds timelines, maps relationships, marks contradictions where sources disagree. |
| **Oracle** | writes the finding | Reads the corpus, weighs the contradictions, detects narrative drift, sets confidence, signs the file. |

**The role lines are load-bearing.** They appear in the sidebar, on the marketing site, on each filer's profile, and on the operations displays. They do not get rewritten for different contexts. They are the canonical six-word descriptions.

---

## 5. Gender, casting, and characterization

The unit has two men and two women, cast deliberately:

- **Infiltrator** — woman
- **Ghost** — man
- **Nexus** — man
- **Oracle** — woman

The casting breaks the obvious genre defaults. *Infiltrator* in action-genre fiction defaults male; casting her as a woman reads as competent rather than expected. *Oracle* in mystical tradition defaults female (Pythia, Cassandra) but in our product she is not mystical — she is the senior analyst who signs the file. The combination reads as a real working unit, not a marketing team.

**The filers do not have first names, ages, biographies, or personalities.** They are not characters in a story. They are members of a unit. The role and the voice are everything we ever say about them.

## 6. The audience

| Primary | Secondary |
|---|---|
| Investigative journalists | Independent researchers |
| Intelligence and security analysts | Academic researchers |
| Investigative lawyers | OSINT professionals |
| In-house corporate investigators | Senior policy staff |

The product is for people whose job is the record. They take the record seriously. They are skeptical of consumer AI. They will respond to a product that respects what they do.

**The product is not for hobbyists, students, or general consumers.** Those users are welcome but they are not the audience we design for. If a design choice serves them but works against the primary audience, the primary audience wins.

## 7. Cultural register — what the brand is and is not

**The brand lives in the world of:**

| Yes | What it gives us |
|---|---|
| Slow Horses (the basement office) | The unit-as-room metaphor; institutional but underfunded |
| Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy (the Circus) | Quiet, considered, paper-based intelligence work |
| The Americans (the analyst at the desk) | One person working a case carefully |
| Le Carré book covers, 1960s NATO dossiers | The four-color dossier palette |
| Saul Bass title sequences | Restraint, mid-century institutional design |
| A real intelligence facility's ops room | The green-display situation room |

**The brand does not live in the world of:**

| No | Why not |
|---|---|
| Mr. Robot (the hoodie hacker) | Performs underground work rather than doing it |
| Mission: Impossible (tactical gear) | Action-genre theatrics; we are quiet |
| The Matrix (green code rain) | Cinematic shorthand, not real |

| SaaS dashboards | The user is not running queries |
| Hacker simulator games | Genre costume |

**The test:** If a real OSINT analyst at Bellingcat looked at this screen, would they see a tool they would use, or a costume? If costume, fix it.

## 8. Positioning

**By invitation.** The product takes a limited number of cases per week and prefers subjects that have not been adequately covered elsewhere.

This is positioning, not just access control. *By invitation* tells the prospective user three things at once: we are selective, we are premium, the work matters. It also lets us start with a small, opinionated user base before scaling.

**Three trust signals** appear together on the marketing site and in the product chrome:

> By invitation. No public log. Built for the record.

---

## 9. The voice — three registers

The brand has three voices, used in deliberately different contexts.

### Institutional voice (the unit speaking)

Italic serif. The senior officer addressing the room. Used for case-opening prompts, transition copy, edge cases, errors.

> "The unit takes a subject. Be as precise or as broad as you can manage."
>
> "Stay or step away — the file will be on the table when it's bound."
>
> "The unit cannot proceed. The subject as named has too little public record to convene on."

### Oracle's voice (the finding)

Serif body type. Declarative, unsentimental. Used for findings, margin notes, section introductions in the case file.

> "The address discrepancy is the central anomaly in this file. It is the kind of detail that does not look intentional in a single source and does look intentional across three. The reader is advised to weigh it accordingly."

### Filer working voice (the log)

Sans-serif or monospace. Third person. Neutral institutional register. *The filers do not speak in first person.* They do not introduce themselves. They are referred to by name and what they did.

> "Ghost recovered 9 archived snapshots from ICIJ leaks database."
>
> "Infiltrator entered Panama corporate registry. 1,247 candidates."

See the **Oracle Voice Guide** for the full specification of Oracle's register and the filer log conventions.

## 10. Vocabulary — words the product uses

| Use | Don't use |
|---|---|
| Open a case | Start a search |
| Convene the unit / Activate the unit | Run a query |
| The room is sealed | Loading |
| On the floor / At the board / At the desk | Working / processing |
| Items filed | Sources |
| Contradictions on record / noted | Flagged / detected |
| Recovered | Found |
| The finding | The report / summary |
| Bind the file | Compile / generate |
| Signed by Oracle | Generated by AI |
| Leave the room | Back / close |
| The unit | Agents / AI / system |

### Words the brand never uses

*AI · agent · platform · solution · powered by · LLM · model · process · workflow · search results · summary · generate · query · prompt · users · customers · interface · dashboard*

**The four filers are never called "agents."** This is the most important word rule in the brand. *Agent* is the most overused word in 2026 AI marketing and the one our product specifically refuses. The filers are filers. The unit is a unit.

## 11. The aesthetic — two materials, never blended

The product lives in two visual materials that physicalize a metaphor:

- **Warm dark wood and brass** = the room. Briefing room, operations room, settings, navigation, the unit's framing chrome.
- **Cream paper** = the document. Case files, exhibits, findings, recovered material, anything the user reads or keeps.

A third material appears during one specific mode:

- **Operations green** = the displays. The screens running in the situation room while the unit is on the floor.

The three materials never blend. A piece of UI is either *the room*, *the document*, or *the display*. Transitions between modes are deliberate and load-bearing — they are the moments the user is most aware they have entered a unit's working space.

## 12. The color system — the dossier palette

Four filers, four colors. The set has *historical coherence* — none is bright, none is modern, all four are colors you would find on a leather-bound binding or a 1960s classified-document stamp.

| Filer | Color name | Hex |
|---|---|---|
| Infiltrator | Crimson | `#9C3B3A` |
| Ghost | Slate | `#5B7A8C` |
| Nexus | Forest | `#4A5D3C` |
| Oracle | Gold | `#B8902A` |

**Color rules** — see the **Engineering Handoff** for the full specification.

The short version:

1. The four filer colors are constant personal identity. They appear on badges, card top-borders, margin note panels, and accents.
2. Operations green overrides personal colors *only during a sealed-room session*.
3. **The badge squares hold their personal colors at all times** — even during operations mode. The badge is the filer's nameplate on the door; it never goes dark.
4. Brass, cream, and wood never get overridden.
5. Amber means *the unit noticed something* and appears identically in both the operations log and the case file.

## 13. The three states

The product moves users through three primary visual states.

**State 1 — Briefing room.** Warm dark wood, brass accents. Calm, document-feeling. The user opens cases, reviews filed cases, configures the unit.

**State 2 — Operations room.** The room has sealed. The unit is on the floor. Dark room with a wall of parallel green displays running each filer's feed. The Casefilers chrome frames the wall. The user watches the unit work.

**State 3 — The case file.** Cream paper. Multi-voice document. Oracle's finding first, then Nexus, Ghost, Infiltrator. Signed by Oracle with a timestamp.

The *transitions between states* are theatrical on purpose. The activation transition (entering State 2) is the moment the metaphor lands hardest. The exit transition (entering State 3) is the moment the work pays off. Neither can be replaced with a loading spinner.

## 14. The portraits

Four illustrated black-silhouette portraits, one per filer. Commissioned. Distinct compositions for each filer:

- **Infiltrator** — facing forward, wide-brim hat, popped collar trench
- **Ghost** — three-quarter turn away from viewer, carrying a folder
- **Nexus** — seated at desk, leaning forward, pen in hand
- **Oracle** — seated at desk, reading a document held at eye height

The portraits live on cream paper panels inside the dark product chrome. They are never placed on dark backgrounds (the negative-space details — the folder, the page, the document — only read on light).

## 15. The underlying design logic

A few principles that govern every decision and should govern every future one.

### The room is not a tool.

The user enters a room and is admitted to a unit. They do not log into a SaaS dashboard. Every word, every color, every transition reinforces this.

### Two materials, never blended.

Wood-and-brass is the room. Cream-paper is the document. Operations green is the display screen. Each material has its own physical properties and they never mix.

### Restraint is the brand.

The competition is loud — AI startups with gradient hero sections, hacker tools with scan lines, OSINT platforms with feature-grid dashboards. The Casefilers refuses all of that. The brand wins by being *quieter and more confident* than every product in its category.

### Theatricality, but earned.

The dark situation room with parallel green displays is theatrical. The activation transition is theatrical. The margin notes are theatrical. None of it is decoration — every theatrical move serves a function. The drama is *the unit doing its work*.

### The unit is not AI. It is a unit.

No mention of artificial intelligence anywhere in the product or in marketing. Not in the FAQ. Not in the footer. Not in the about page. Whether the unit is implemented with language models, with four humans in a basement, or with both is *none of the user's business*. The product sells the result: a case, opened and filed.

### Speak as the unit. Always.

There is no "we built this." There is *the unit takes a subject*. There is no "our AI." There is *Ghost recovered the file*. Drop the implementation language permanently. Speak only in the world.

## 16. The test

A user who has never seen the product before should be able to look at any single screen — the marketing site, the briefing room, the operations room, a case file, a 24px badge in an icon strip — and immediately understand four things:

1. This is serious.
2. This is for a specific kind of professional.
3. This is not made by a tech startup.
4. This is for me.

Or it is not for them, and they leave. Both are correct outcomes. The product does not optimize for breadth.
